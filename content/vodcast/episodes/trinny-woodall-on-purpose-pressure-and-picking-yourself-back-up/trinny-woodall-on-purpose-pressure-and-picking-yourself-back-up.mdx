Trinny Woodall starts a lot of mornings with her head in a basin of ice. Not for wellness theatre, but because, in her words, she wakes up and her face looks like a crumpled bed of nettles and she needs her brain to switch on. It is exactly the kind of detail that makes her so easy to love. Glamorous and entirely without pretence, in the same breath.

You might know [Trinny Woodall](https://www.instagram.com/trinnywoodall/) as the co-host of *What Not To Wear*, or as the founder of Trinny London, the beauty brand now worth hundreds of millions. But what she has really mastered is something quieter and harder to buy. She knows how to back herself.

She was the very first guest I recorded for Beautiful Inside, and two years on she came back to talk about what business, reinvention and a few brutal years have taught her.

## A Word Invented By a Man

Trinny has no time for imposter syndrome. None.

> "I hate the word imposter syndrome. I feel it was a word invented by a man."

Her reframe is the most useful thing I have heard on the subject. If you walk into a room and feel you do not belong, she says, it is almost always because you do not yet have the knowledge everyone else is using. So go and get it.

She still does it herself. In an intense financial meeting she will quietly type an unfamiliar acronym into her phone rather than nod along and pretend. The question she asks is simple: what do I need to learn so I can come back in the room and have a voice. That is the whole trick. Not confidence as a personality trait, but curiosity as a practice.

She is just as deliberate about how she walks in. She wants to enter the room as a person, she says, not as a woman fundraising in a world where only two per cent of capital goes to women.

## Lift Each Other Up, or Step Away

The theme Trinny keeps returning to is kindness, and its opposite, drama. Women, she says, can choose to lift each other up or pull each other down.

It goes back to boarding school. At twelve or thirteen, desperate to be included, she traded gossip for belonging, until the day it hurt someone and stopped her cold. She has lived by a quiet rule ever since, and passed it to her daughter Lyla on her own way to boarding school. Whenever you feel it, step away from the drama.

She gravitates towards the people who never need it. The cousin who only ever had a good word for anyone. The friends who pull you up rather than down. She keeps very different friendship groups on purpose, so she is never caught inside any one cycle.

## The Years She Would Not Give Back

Trinny got clean from addiction in her twenties, after two stints in rehab and years of daily meetings. She has spoken often about losing Lyla's father. None of it is performed for sympathy. It is simply the material her resilience is made from.

> "I don't regret my years when I had a tough time because it's made me what I am today."

The pain fades, she says, the way it does after childbirth. You don't remember how much it hurt, you just remember that you have your child. What is left is empathy, the thing that makes her write personally to a Trinny London employee going through something hard, because she has almost certainly been there too.

## Showing Up, For Real

With a community as devoted as the Trinny Tribe, showing up every day could feel like a tax. For Trinny it is closer to a transfusion. When she is flat she goes live, because anxiety always feels worse when you think you are the only one carrying it.

The authenticity is deliberate. When she picks up her phone she pictures specific women down the lens, Joanna in Sydney, Mary in America, and sends her energy to them. Nothing is read off notes behind the camera. And when she shares something tender, her mother's Alzheimer's, the strange emptiness of Lyla leaving for university, she treats it as generosity rather than exposure. If it helps one woman feel less alone, it was worth saying.

## Trinny's Sacred Six (That Became Eleven)

We always finish with the Sacred Six, the products and practices that make a guest feel their best. Trinny, being Trinny, could not stop at six.

- A David G morning meditation on Insight Timer, twenty-two minutes to set the day's intention
- Weight training three times a week, plus two sessions of yoga, to protect her bones through menopause
- SPF every single day, layered under [her own Skin Perfector](https://www.trinnylondon.com) for a bit of glow on no sleep
- A basin of ice for the mornings her face needs reviving, an old Hollywood trick
- Hot water and lemon first thing, every day, for thirty-five years
- [Take Back Time](https://www.trinnylondon.com), the repair elixir she keeps in every handbag
- Bunny hops for lymphatic drainage, facial fascia massage, and creatine and MCT oil for energy and brain

> "I want to look after my body so it looks after me."

After an hour with her, you believe she will.

## Why It Stayed With Me

Trinny makes backing yourself look like a decision rather than a gift. Learn the thing that scares you. Choose kindness over noise. Keep the hard years, because that is where the substance lives. I left this one ready to put my own head in a bucket of ice. I suspect you might too.
