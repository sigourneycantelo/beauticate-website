The most popular skincare content on my Instagram in the last year hasn't been about a new product. It's been about a technique. Specifically, the question of whether you're applying your skincare correctly — and for most people, the answer is no.

Skin flooding sounds more dramatic than it is. It's simply the practice of layering hydrating products onto *damp* skin, in a specific sequence, to maximise moisture retention. But the reason it works — and the reason it produces noticeably different results than the same products applied dry — comes down to basic chemistry that most skincare brands have never bothered to explain.

## The problem with how most people apply moisturiser

Humectants are the workhorses of modern hydration skincare. Hyaluronic acid, glycerin, beta-glucan, sodium PCA — they're all humectants, and their job is to attract and hold water molecules. The issue is that humectants need a water source to draw from. Apply them to completely dry skin in a dry environment and they'll draw moisture from the deeper layers of your dermis — which is exactly the opposite of what you want.

The other problem: most skincare routines have a gap between application and sealing. You apply your serum, scroll through your phone for three minutes, then apply your moisturiser. By then, a significant amount of what you just applied has evaporated. Particularly in air-conditioned offices or centrally-heated bedrooms, transepidermal water loss is rapid and constant.

Skin flooding solves both problems simultaneously.

## The exact method

**Step 1: Cleanse, but don't dry completely.**
After cleansing, pat your face to remove dripping water but leave skin visibly damp — like a light mist rather than wet. This is the water source your humectants need.

**Step 2: Apply your humectant layer immediately.**
While skin is still damp, apply a hydrating essence, toner, or serum with hyaluronic acid or glycerin. Press it gently into skin — don't rub, which creates friction and encourages evaporation.

**Step 3: Add a second humectant layer before the first dries.**
A second serum or ampoule goes on while the first is still damp. You're building a reservoir of water-attracting ingredients.

**Step 4: Seal immediately with your moisturiser.**
While everything is still damp, apply your moisturiser — ideally one with ceramides or fatty acids that form an occlusive seal. This traps everything underneath and prevents evaporation.

**Step 5 (daytime): SPF.**
Sealed under SPF, your skin will hold that hydration for hours. The dewy effect people associate with "glass skin" is simply well-retained moisture.

The whole sequence should take under two minutes. The key is not stopping between steps.

## What to use

Not all products work equally well for skin flooding. You want:

**Humectant layer:** A lightweight essence or serum with multiple humectants at different molecular weights. Low molecular weight hyaluronic acid penetrates; high molecular weight sits on the surface and seals. Both in the same formula is the ideal. The Ordinary Hyaluronic Acid 2% + B5 is the value option I've used for years.

**Sealing layer:** Look for ceramides, fatty acids, or squalane — these mimic your skin's own barrier lipids and seal without blocking pores. The Tatcha Dewy Skin Cream is my current favourite for the flooding seal step because the texture is perfectly calibrated to lock in moisture without heaviness.

**Avoid:** Pure petroleum-based occlusives (Vaseline, heavy balms) on the face during the day — they're too occlusive for most skin types and will cause congestion.

## Who it's actually for

Every skin type. I need to say that clearly because "hydration" has been incorrectly coded as a dry skin concern. Oily skin is frequently dehydrated — the oil and the water content of skin are separate systems. Dehydrated oily skin often overproduces sebum in response to the dehydration, creating a cycle that skin flooding can actually help break.

If your skin feels tight after cleansing, looks dull in photos, shows surface texture that isn't quite acne or dryness, or if your makeup oxidises faster than it should, you are probably dehydrated regardless of your skin type.

The chemistry is sound. The results are genuine. And the technique costs nothing — it's a reframe of what you're already doing.
