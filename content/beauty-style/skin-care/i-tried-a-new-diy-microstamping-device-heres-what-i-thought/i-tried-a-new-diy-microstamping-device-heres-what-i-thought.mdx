If you’ve missed the dermarolling and stamping movement that’s taken the skincare world by storm, then where on earth have you been? The idea of rolling or stamping your skin with tiny needles to create rejuvenating micro trauma and help products better penetrate has become de rigeur in dermal circles. It’s even filtered down to the masses with leading skincare brands launching their own home dermarollers alongside serums to encourage the practice at home. These tools come with their own set of caveats, however. The main concern being that, in the uneducated hand, the needles can cause much more damage than intended. According to many leading dermatologists, dermarollers can drag the skin and create uneven punctures. There’s also a risk of infection if they’re not done in sterile-enough conditions.

Intrigued, I decided to try it before a beauty event I had coming up. You start by twisting open the chamber of the device and filling until it’s half full with one (1) ampule of serum, then you leave it upside down for 2 minutes while it travels into the needles. This is when you wash your face and hands to ensure it’s as sterile an experience as possible. Then you start stamping! You start from the centre of your face outwards, using a 50% overlap technique.

![Sigourney Cantelo pressing the Qure micro-infusion stamp across her cheek](/content/beauty-style/skin-care/i-tried-a-new-diy-microstamping-device-heres-what-i-thought/IMG_2023.jpg)

This will create a controlled wound healing response in the skin which in turn prompts your cells to begin collagen production. It also creates micro channels in the skin that help penetrate the skin barrier, helping the serums to penetrate more deeply. When you’re finished simply pat any leftover serum into the skin – all the way down to your neck. The result? Apparently it will lead to improved elasticity, smoother fine lines, and diminished wrinkles. I think of it like a workout for your skin, encouraging it to rejuvenate and revitalize itself.

I found it quite prickly – particularly on the upper lip! But it doesn’t exactly *hurt* because the needles are finer than human hair. It’s slightly more intense than dermarolling but not nearly as ouchy as the in clinic procedures. Afterwards my skin was pink and pricked in places but it cleared up by morning and my skin was glowing. The glow increased as the day progressed and by day three I was admiring my complexion whenever I passed a mirror.
