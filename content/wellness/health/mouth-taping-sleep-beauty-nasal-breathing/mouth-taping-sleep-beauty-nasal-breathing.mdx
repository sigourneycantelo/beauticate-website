I need you to know that I resisted this for a long time.

I first heard about mouth taping from a functional medicine practitioner whose opinion I respect enormously. I filed it under "things that are probably fine but I'm not ready to explain to my husband." That was eighteen months ago. I finally started because I was frustrated with the quality of my sleep — not the duration, which was fine, but that heavy-headed, slightly puffy-faced morning feeling that more sleep didn't seem to fix.

Ninety days in, here's an honest account of what happened.

## Why nasal breathing during sleep matters

The case for nasal breathing begins with nitric oxide — a molecule produced in the nasal passages that plays a remarkable number of roles in the body. Nitric oxide dilates blood vessels, improving circulation and oxygen delivery to cells. It has antimicrobial properties, killing pathogens before they reach the lungs. It regulates blood pressure. And it signals the blood's haemoglobin to release oxygen more efficiently to tissues.

When you mouth breathe, you bypass the nitric oxide production entirely. You also bypass the nasal passages' function of filtering, humidifying, and warming air — which means drier airways, a higher pathogen load reaching the lungs, and disrupted sleep architecture because mouth breathing is associated with more frequent micro-arousals (brief awakenings you don't remember but that fragment your sleep cycles).

The research on the relationship between nasal breathing and sleep quality is more substantive than I expected. James Nestor's book *Breath* catalysed mainstream interest in this, but the underlying science predates the wellness conversation by decades. Chronic mouth breathing is associated with worse sleep quality, increased snoring, higher rates of sleep apnea, more frequent upper respiratory infections, and — here's where it becomes a beauty conversation — chronically elevated cortisol from fragmented sleep.

## What I actually noticed

**Week one:** Nothing except increased awareness of my own breathing, which is mildly anxiety-inducing. I used a small vertical strip of 3M Micropore tape — the white paper surgical tape — placed over the centre of my lips. I expected to tear it off in the night. I didn't.

**Weeks two to four:** I started waking up feeling like my mouth wasn't a desert. This sounds trivial but it was meaningful — the dry mouth I'd normalised as just a thing that happens was apparently entirely a result of mouth breathing.

**Week six:** Someone asked if I'd changed something. My skin, they said. I hadn't introduced any new products. I was using the same routine I'd had for months.

**Week twelve:** The morning puffiness I'd accepted as my baseline has substantially reduced. I look more defined in the morning — the jaw, the under-eye area. I have more energy before my first coffee than I used to. My sleep tracker shows more time in deep sleep.

Could these be placebo effects? Some of them, possibly. The mechanism for the puffiness reduction is genuinely plausible though — better sleep quality means more efficient lymphatic drainage overnight, and reduced mouth breathing means less dehydration-driven water retention.

## The skin connection

Here's what I believe is happening, drawing on conversations with our wellness editor Brooke Stevenson and my own reading of the research:

Better nasal breathing → better sleep architecture → lower cortisol → less inflammation and collagen degradation during sleep.

Better nitric oxide production → improved microcirculation → better nutrient delivery to skin cells → improved cellular repair overnight.

Less mouth breathing → less dehydration → better-maintained skin barrier → reduced morning tightness and reactivity.

None of these are dramatic effects in isolation. Together, over weeks and months, they produce a quality to skin that I can see in photos but struggle to attribute to any single cause.

## The practical reality

It takes about a week to stop noticing the tape. After that it becomes routine — part of the same sequence as applying my overnight skincare (I use the Sunday Riley Luna as my last step, then tape). 

If you have a deviated septum, significant seasonal allergies, or any degree of sleep apnea, please talk to a doctor before trying this. For everyone else, the barrier to trying it is genuinely low: one roll of surgical tape from the chemist and three nights of experimentation will tell you whether it's something your body takes to.

I can't promise it will change your skin. I can tell you it changed mine, and that the mechanisms for why are more credible than most of the things I've spent significant money on in this industry.

Start with three nights. See how you feel in the morning. That's all I'm suggesting.
