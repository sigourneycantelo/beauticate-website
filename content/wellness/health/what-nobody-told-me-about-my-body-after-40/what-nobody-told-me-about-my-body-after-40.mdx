There was a moment, somewhere in my early forties, when I realised that everything had simply stopped working.

Not dramatically. Not all at once. Just quietly, consistently, in ways I couldn't quite explain. The workouts that used to keep me lean weren't working anymore. I was eating better than ever but still bloated. Sleep was a joke. My mood was, let's say, not my finest. And the people closest to me were noticing.

It was during Covid that I really started talking about it properly with my business partner Kate. We'd been spending so much time together we couldn't blame stress alone. After many walks and many conversations, we started digging. I grew up with a naturopath mother. Kate had a GP father. We started asking questions.

What we found shocked us. And eventually, it changed everything.

![Lisa Walker, co-founder of Eir Women, smiling against a pink background](/content/wellness/health/what-nobody-told-me-about-my-body-after-40/lisa-walker-portrait.jpg)

## Nobody was talking about it. And when they were, nobody was listening.

The first thing that hit me was how dismissed women were. The products on the market were clearly not designed by women like us. The supplement category was overwhelming and largely useless. And perimenopause - this years-long transition that precedes menopause - was barely spoken about at all. When it was, it was framed as something to simply endure.

Symptoms like fatigue, brain fog, weight gain, low mood and disrupted sleep were being written off as 'just getting older.' Women were gaslit by doctors and being told to 'just deal with it'. That was what made me truly angry.

And we weren't alone. Research Eir Women conducted across 322 Australian women aged 40 to 60 found that 62% felt under-informed about how perimenopause and menopause affect metabolism and weight, with many expressing frustration at the absence of guidance on what to actually do about it. Nearly a third of women aged 41 to 50 strongly agreed they felt overwhelmed - not occasionally, but as a baseline state of being.

## What actually moves the needle - and what doesn't.

The biggest mistake I see women making in their forties is thinking they need to try harder. More workouts. Less food. More discipline. In reality, that approach often makes things worse. More stress, more cortisol, more hormonal disruption, more burnout. You cannot out-supplement or out-HRT a chaotic lifestyle.

If I had to distil it down, here's what I'd tell every woman in her forties:

- **Sleep first.** It's the foundation of everything. No sleep means no hormone balance and very little in the way of coping mechanisms. Everything else sits on top of this.
- **Strength training over punishing cardio.** Muscle is everything after 40. I love pole dancing personally - it's strength work that also brings genuine joy, which matters more than people give it credit for.
- **Blood sugar balance.** Wildly underrated. Stable blood sugar means stable energy, fewer cravings and better mood. It changes everything.
- **Stress management** - not stress elimination, which is impossible, but building the resilience to down-regulate. High cortisol will undo every good thing you do. This one cannot be skipped.

The research backs this up. Among midlife Australian women surveyed, those who engaged in regular physical activity, prioritised hormonal health through nutrition and supplements, and practised mindfulness consistently reported lower anxiety, better sleep and improved emotional resilience. It's not just one thing. It's the combination, done consistently over time.

## The trust problem nobody wants to admit.

One of the most striking findings from Eir Women's research was this: the issue for midlife women is not access to healthcare. It's *trust*. More than a quarter of respondents strongly agreed that finding trustworthy health advice is difficult. Women in their forties - actively managing perimenopausal symptoms, often at their most health-engaged - reported the lowest trust levels of all.

*'I have access to doctors, but I don't always feel they take my concerns seriously, especially about menopause.'*

That quote came from a survey respondent. I have heard versions of it hundreds of times.

That gap between what women are experiencing and what they're being told is part of why so many of us ended up in digital communities, researching alone at midnight, or trying things that didn't work. It's also why Kate and I felt so strongly that Eir Women needed to be rooted in real science, communicated honestly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is perimenopause and when does it start?**
A: Perimenopause is the years-long transition that precedes menopause, typically beginning in a woman's early to mid-forties. Symptoms include fatigue, brain fog, weight changes, disrupted sleep, low mood and irregular cycles. Many women experience these symptoms for years before receiving an explanation or diagnosis.

**Q: Is creatine good for women over 40?**
A: Yes - creatine is one of the most well-researched supplements available and is particularly relevant for women over 40. At 5g daily, it supports muscle mass preservation, brain function, cellular energy production and bone health. Most women have never considered it because it has historically been associated with male athletes, but the evidence for women in midlife is strong.

**Q: Why do women feel dismissed by doctors about perimenopause?**
A: Eir Women research across 322 Australian women found that more than a quarter strongly agreed that finding trustworthy health advice is difficult, and those most actively managing perimenopausal symptoms reported the lowest trust levels of all. Symptoms like fatigue, brain fog and mood changes are frequently attributed to "just getting older" rather than investigated.

**Q: What actually works for women's health after 40?**
A: According to Lisa Walker and the Eir Women research, the fundamentals that consistently make the most difference are: prioritising sleep above everything else, strength training over punishing cardio, blood sugar balance for stable energy and mood, and building stress resilience rather than trying to eliminate stress. No supplement or HRT will compensate for a chaotic lifestyle.
