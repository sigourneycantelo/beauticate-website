The best family resorts in Nusa Dua, Bali have always had a problem: they do families brilliantly, or they do adults beautifully, but rarely both at once. Paradisus by Meliá Bali — the newly transformed luxury all-inclusive resort on Nusa Dua's sherbet sands, and the first of its kind in Asia — is making a serious case for both. Sigourney checked in to see if it delivered.

## The Welcome Ceremony That Sets the Tone

I close my eyes as the priest begins to chant. The air is thick with frangipani and incense. Smoke curls upwards through a canopy of coconut palms, dissipating into the morning light. Somewhere above us, birds chirrup and palm fronds clatter softly in the breeze. And then, my eye catches a swift movement above us: a squirrel darting up the ancient stone shrine, pausing for a moment as though he too has come to pay his respects.

<Portrait src="/content/destinations/travel/paradisus-bali-melia-review/canang-sari.jpg" alt="Canang sari flower offering being made by hand at Paradisus Bali welcome ceremony" side="left" />

Kneeling in a sarong and sash on cobbled stone of the temple, I hold my *canang sari* — a small square tray I've just made (badly) from *janur*, young coconut palm leaves. Inside: grains of rice, petals of frangipani and marigold, a sliver of pandan, a slice of banana. The priest looks like he's seen decades of early mornings and open air. He has eyes that seem permanently on the verge of laughing at something only he can see. The other journalists bow their heads. I follow.

Our welcome ceremony takes place in the gardens of Paradisus by Meliá Bali, which has just emerged from a significant renovation and is pitching itself as the ultimate Bali luxury family holiday. At this particular moment, though, none of that is the point. What strikes me is the silence within the sound. The chanting, the birds, the leaves — all of it present, layered, alive — and underneath it, a profound stillness. I haven't sat still like this for a very long time.

![Balinese house and temple gardens at Paradisus by Meliá Bali](/content/destinations/travel/paradisus-bali-melia-review/balinese-house.jpg)

After his incantation, the priest sits with us and speaks about Tri Hita Karana, the Balinese understanding of wellbeing as a balance between people, nature and the divine. *Sekala* and *niskala*, the seen world and the unseen. We are all connected, he says. What we put out returns to us. It's not a complex philosophy. It's also not one that's easy to remember when you're at home answering emails at ten-thirty at night. And yet, somewhere between the incense and the rustling of leaves, it lands. I walk away feeling calmer than I have in weeks.

![Main pool area at Paradisus by Meliá Bali with white loungers](/content/destinations/travel/paradisus-bali-melia-review/main-pool.jpg)

## Why Nusa Dua — and Why This Resort in Particular

Located on the sherbet-sand peninsula of Nusa Dua — the name means "two islands" in Balinese, a nod to the two small headlands that define this southern coastline — Paradisus by Meliá Bali marks the brand's first foray into Asia. The resort draws its philosophy from what it calls Wellness Designed by Destination, which sounds exactly like the kind of phrase that should make a travel writer's eyes glaze over. Thankfully, it delivers considerably more than wellness buzzwords.

## Inside Paradisus: What 40 Years and a Full Renovation Looks Like

The building that became Paradisus has more history than its fresh renovation might suggest. Meliá Bali opened in 1985 as the first Meliá hotel outside Spain: a Majorcan hospitality group betting on a stretch of southern Bali that had barely registered on the international tourist map. At the time, the area had not yet attained its later popularity. The resort that rose from what had been coconut groves and fishing villages became the anchor of what is now one of Asia's most concentrated strips of five-star real estate. Forty years on, the same Spanish group has done it again — reinvented the property as the first Paradisus in Asia, its luxury all-inclusive brand better known until now from Mexico and the Caribbean. There's a pleasing circularity to that. Bali was where Meliá first took a risk. It turns out Bali is still worth betting on.

<Portrait src="/content/destinations/travel/paradisus-bali-melia-review/sante-breakfast.jpg" alt="Breakfast at Santé restaurant, The Reserve at Paradisus Bali" side="right" />

The property is enormous. Set amongst sprawling tropical gardens threaded with lotus ponds and sandstone water channels, it unfolds through open-air walkways rather than the usual carpeted hotel corridors. Ancient beringin — the sacred fig trees the Balinese wrap in *saput poleng*, the black-and-white checked cloth that signals a living spirit within — cast cooling shadows across the paths. White cempaka trees, a large-flowered cousin of the magnolia, release their heavy fragrance in the morning heat. More than five hundred coconut palms stand throughout the grounds — not as decoration but as the literal heartwood of Balinese life, their fronds stripped daily to weave the *janur* for offerings placed on every threshold, every shrine, every doorstep across the island.

![Aquazone water park at Paradisus by Meliá Bali, Nusa Dua](/content/destinations/travel/paradisus-bali-melia-review/aquazone.jpg)

Even finding your room feels like a small adventure. At check-in, a fresh coconut arrives etched with my name. Later, wandering towards my suite, I notice messages pressed carefully into smooth sand displays along the pathways. Birthdays. Anniversaries. Family names. It's a tiny gesture in a very large resort. It works.

## The Reserve: A Resort Within the Resort

![The Reserve Junior Suite with Lagoon Access at Paradisus Bali](/content/destinations/travel/paradisus-bali-melia-review/reserve-suite-lagoon.jpg)

I stay in one of The Reserve Lagoon Access Suites, the adults-only enclave for guests over sixteen. The rooms have been thoughtfully refreshed — cream tones, warm timber, quiet Balinese accents — but the real reason to book is what's outside the sliding doors: direct access to a winding lagoon pool that snakes through the gardens like a sparkling turquoise river. Morning swims become non-negotiable. Sunset cocktails follow naturally.

![The Reserve Pool at Paradisus by Meliá Bali](/content/destinations/travel/paradisus-bali-melia-review/reserve-pool-pr.jpg)

The Reserve is, arguably, the smartest thing the resort has done.

Meliá Bali has been beloved by families for decades. What it has now managed — and it's harder than it sounds — is creating a resort that works equally well for adults who would rather not hear other people's children at breakfast. The Reserve has its own pool, its own beach zone, its own dining experiences and a level of personalised service that tips the whole thing towards boutique. At the Reserve Pool, an attendant quietly cleans my sunglasses while I'm swimming. It is completely unnecessary. It is precisely why it feels luxurious.

![Family Concierge Junior Suite with Lagoon Access at Paradisus Bali](/content/destinations/travel/paradisus-bali-melia-review/family-suite-lagoon.jpg)

## For Families: The Kind of Holiday Where Someone Else Does the Work

What's happening beyond that peaceful bubble, though, is a different holiday entirely. Dedicated Family Concierge suites. Two kids' clubs. A teen club. Water slides. A water park — one of the only ones in the area. Karaoke rooms, outdoor movies, marshmallow roasting, bunk beds in forest-themed family rooms. For a certain kind of exhausted parent, this is very close to paradise. The kind of holiday where the children are occupied and happy and someone else has organised it all.

## The All-Inclusive Question — and Why This One Is Different

I'll admit I arrived sceptical of the all-inclusive concept. My previous experiences have generally involved wristbands, buffet fatigue and the suspicion that everything good costs extra. Paradisus takes a different approach. Hungry at eleven? Eat. Room service at midnight? Done. Mini bar replenished daily. Twenty-four-hour room service included. All eight restaurants part of the package. The result is something genuinely disarming: you stop doing mental arithmetic. You stop tracking the bill. You simply settle in. As anyone who has ever organised a family holiday will understand, that alone may be worth the price of admission.

![Pool salads at Paradisus by Meliá Bali — fresh food by the pool](/content/destinations/travel/paradisus-bali-melia-review/salads.jpg)

The food is better than it has any right to be at this scale. Tokimeku, the Japanese restaurant, is the standout — pristine sashimi, miso cod of the kind you'd drive across Sydney for, robatayaki dishes that arrive with quiet theatrical precision. Samira, the Middle Eastern restaurant, leans into fragrant spices and dishes designed for sharing. For Reserve guests, Santé is where mornings belong: no small people in sight, coffee arriving promptly, tropical fruit gleaming in the early light, pastries disappearing faster than intended. And salads by the pool for lunch that don't feel boring.

![Kanna Beach Restaurant at Paradisus Bali — beach dining at sunset](/content/destinations/travel/paradisus-bali-melia-review/kanna-beach.jpg)

Evenings tend to begin at Kanna Beach Restaurant, where the sky over the Indian Ocean dissolves through pink and coral and gold into something almost embarrassingly beautiful.

## YHI Spa: Two Hours and Twenty Minutes of Bali Doing What Bali Does Best

![YHI Spa at Paradisus by Meliá Bali](/content/destinations/travel/paradisus-bali-melia-review/yhi-spa.jpg)

<YouTubeEmbed url="https://youtube.com/shorts/rDmc_sydnN4" caption="YHI Spa at Paradisus by Meliá Bali" />

One afternoon I make my way to YHI Spa, set within its own leafy sanctuary open to the warm air. A towel infused with sweet orange is pressed into my hands. Rosella tea follows. Then comes two hours and twenty minutes of Balinese massage that, somewhere in its middle section, achieves what I can only describe as a full system reset. I emerge lighter. Quieter. Rather surprised. Bali working its magic yet again.

## What Paradisus Gets Right

Throughout my stay, I return to a conversation with Tasya Aulia, the resort's Director of Marketing, who showed me a vast mural that has hung in the resort since it first opened — humans, animals, the divine, all interwoven — which survived the renovation intact. It wasn't an accident. The resort could have replaced it with something fresher, something more content-friendly. Instead they kept it. A decision that tells you something.

![Lobby of Paradisus by Meliá Bali with original resort mural](/content/destinations/travel/paradisus-bali-melia-review/lobby.jpg)

It's easy, in Bali, to mistake performance for culture. The island has so many experiences that look exactly right for a photograph that it can be hard to know what's real. The ceremony on my first morning felt real. The priest's unhurried stillness felt real. The mural felt real. Not everything here is designed for your grid.

<Portrait src="/content/destinations/travel/paradisus-bali-melia-review/lagoon-pool.jpg" alt="Lagoon pool at The Reserve, Paradisus by Meliá Bali" side="left" />

On my final morning, I wake before the resort does and slip straight from the terrace into the lagoon pool. The gardens are still quiet. The air is warm and slightly green-smelling, the way tropical gardens smell before the heat builds. Birdsong moves through the palms. Beyond the manicured paths and infinity pools, Nusa Dua is stirring. I float on my back and look up through the canopy. The priest's words come back. *Sekala* and *niskala*. The seen and the unseen. The world we move through and the one beneath it, which requires a different kind of attention. Simple ideas. But Bali has always had a talent for making simple things feel important, if you're moving slowly enough to let them. Paradisus won't fix your life. But it might slow you down long enough to remember what it actually contains.
