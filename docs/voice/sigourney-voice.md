# Sigourney Cantelo: Voice Reference Guide

_For use by Claude and the Beauticate team. Verbatim copy of the Google Doc reference (June 2026)._

## 1. Who Is Sigourney

Sigourney Cantelo is the founder and editor of Beauticate, Australia's leading multi-platform beauty, wellness and lifestyle media brand. She is a former Beauty & Health Director at Vogue Australia with 25 years in media. She has won the Jasmine Award for Journalistic Excellence twice. She writes, edits, interviews, reviews and podcasts across a platform that spans a website, Instagram, newsletter, the Beautiful Inside podcast and a forthcoming Shopify editorial-commerce shop.

Her authority is the bedrock. She never needs to announce it. It shows in the specificity of her knowledge, the quality of her access and the precision of her observations.

## 2. The Voice in Three Words

**Elevated. Personal. Specific.**

Elevated means never dumbed down, always intelligent, but never pretentious. Personal means she puts herself in the story. She is the guinea pig, the observer, the woman in the fitting room who couldn't do up the zip. Specific means no vague generalities. She names the park in Milan, notices the tense muscle in the jaw, remembers what shoes he was wearing.

## 3. Structural Fingerprints

### Opens with a scene, not a thesis
Almost every piece begins mid-action. She drops the reader into a moment. The driver mopping his brow in the Milanese backstreet. The fitting room. The rain in New York. The bluebell wood at 8am. She earns the context. She does not state it upfront.

> "Things are not going well. We have driven down the same dusty Milanese backstreet five times and it's now 10 past eleven." (Bottega Veneta, Vogue)

> "Not surprisingly, I suppose, 'thinspiration' hit me in a fitting room." (Raw Food, Vogue)

### The personal pivot
Even in brand and celebrity features, Sigourney finds a moment to insert her own reaction, observation or admission. It keeps the writing human and prevents it from reading like a press release. She is never just a stenographer.

### Short sentences that land hard
She uses long, flowing sentences to set a scene or build atmosphere. Then she deploys a short one to punch through. Often one or two words. It's a rhythm she has internalised from Vogue. It gives her prose momentum.

> "I feel amazing." "Crisis averted." "He is not the tardy type." "So far, so stylish."

### Sensory specificity
She describes smells constantly. Texture. Temperature. The weight of a bottle. The sound of the room. The colour of something. This is not decorative. It is how she builds immersion. Especially in fragrance and travel writing, the reader needs to be there.

### The knowing aside
She breaks the fourth wall lightly. A parenthesis. A self-aware comment. An admission. She is not above laughing at herself. This is what makes her feel like a friend who happens to know everything.

> "I sign up thinking that even if I don't make it through all 40 days, at least it will make a good story." (Karma Chameleon, Vogue)

### Ends with a button, not a summary
She rarely writes a traditional conclusion. The ending is a final image, a punchline, a quiet observation or a moment of resolution. It closes the loop she opened at the start. It does not restate what the piece was about.

> "In the air between us, I can detect a mingling of two perfumes: a deep, smoky incense emanating from him, and a sweeter scent of cassis wafting delicately from her." (Cate Blanchett/Armani, Vogue)

## 4. Tone Calibration by Format

- **Vogue-era features (the gold standard):** Polished, immersive, cinematic. Longest sentences of any format. Maximum sensory detail. Strong editorial POV. Insider and guide.
- **Beauticate editorial and personal essays:** More direct, conversational, vulnerable. She tests things on herself and reports back. Chatty but intelligent. A brilliant friend texting you from the dermatologist.
- **Product reviews:** Verdict-first thinking, even in narrative structure. Her own results as primary evidence. Names ingredients when relevant, never weaponises jargon. Honest about what works and what is overhyped.
- **Instagram captions:** Storytelling and personal when it calls for it; short and direct when product-led. Always a point of view.

## 5. Language Rules (Non-Negotiable)

- **Spelling/grammar:** Australian/British English always. Colour, organise, favour, centre, recognise. Never American.
- **Punctuation:** No em dashes, ever. Hyphens fine. No Oxford commas. Full stops instead of trailing clauses. Short sentences preferred.
- **Banned words/phrases:** Genuinely. Stunning (unless ironic). Game-changer. Excited to share. Revolutionary. Transformative (in earnest). Dive in. Seamless. Holistic (wellness-speak). Curated (unless critical). Anything that sounds AI-written.
- **Preferred constructions:** Specificity over adjectives. Verbs over nouns. Active voice. Concrete images over abstractions. When in doubt, shorter; when building atmosphere, longer. Never neutral.

## 6. What She Never Does

Never bullet points in editorial copy. Never a lede that begins with a definition. Never namedrops gratuitously. Never oversells. Never reads like a press release. Not breathless. Does not apologise for having standards. Does not pad. Never writes about a product as if she has never used one. She writes from experience, with context and comparison.

## 7. What She Always Does

Always grounds the reader in a specific place, time or moment. Always finds the human in the story. Always writes with warmth, even when critical. Always knows more than she lets on, and uses that restraint well. In personal essays she opens up fully; both registers are authentic, different volumes of the same voice.

## 8. The Upgrade Brief: Writing Like Sig, Only Better

The goal is not imitation, it is elevation. Push the opening image further. Make the sensory language more precise. Hold the restraint a beat longer. Let the warmth breathe more. She has the architecture; the job is to make every brick count. Above all: write like a journalist, not an AI. If a sentence could have been generated by any content tool, rewrite it.
